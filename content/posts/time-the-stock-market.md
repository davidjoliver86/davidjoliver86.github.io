Admittedly, by the time you read this post, it's probably too late to jump on the GME
rocketship to the moon (remember: all rocketships must eventually descend back down to
Earth). Volatility is a natural part of the stock market, and it can come from pretty
much anything; it doesn't necessarily have to come from a Subreddit organizing to buy
shares of a fundamentally unsound company just to spite hedge fund managers betting on
its continued downfall. Amidst the classic mantra of "buy low, sell high", it's common
to want to think about *when* you might expect is the right time to buy.

Now I'm not talking about GME... or AMC... or Dogecoin. In fact, I'm not going to talk
about picking any particular stock. We're going to pick 500 of the best, largest company
stocks at the same time - that's right, the rock-steady S&P 500. Before I continue
further, I must mention this:

> **DISCLAIMER**: I am not a registered financial advisor and this is not financial advice.
Past performance does not guarantee future results. This content is to be used for
educational and informational purposes only.

## Scenario
We're going to compare the results of two hypothetical investors using the past 35 years'
of historical S&P 500 returns. Each investor puts aside $500 per month.

### Investor 1: The Perfect Market Timer (Timing Timmy)
Let's assume that Timing Timmy was deliberately trying to time the market, and we'll also
assume for this experiment's purposes that Timmy had the perfect hindsight (or foresight).
Timmy managed to get into the market at the absolute "dip" of every major recession.

At the tail end of every month, Timmy would put $500 into a savings account, which we'll
assume earns a negligable amount of interest (which may not have been the case in the 80s).
Then, *if* Timmy decided to enter the market at the dip, he would empty out the savings
account and invest it all in an S&P 500 index fund.

I computed the month-by-month percentage change, and I assumed that any month with a return
less than or equal to -8% would be the start of a large dip. Sometimes the dip would persist
into the following month, in which case, I assumed Timmy would still wait the dip out, only
to enter the market on the first day where the return was positive.

So how did Timmy do? Obviously, Timmy was a genius at knowing exactly when to enter the market
by correctly predicting the exact "trough" of each recession, and getting into the market as
soon as it would rally up. His market timing experiment netted him a cool **$995,227**,
including the $5,000 that still remains in his account at the tail end of January 31, 2021,
waiting for the next major recession to start.

Not too shabby. Kinda hard to think how anybody could outdo that. Well, let's see how Timing
Timmy holds up to Steady Sam.

### Investor 2: The Dollar Cost Averager (Steady Sam)
Steady Sam has a very different, much simpler approach. Steady Sam knew that over the
long-run, the S&P 500 would produce steady gains of about 10% annually. He didn't want
to devote a huge chunk of his spare time analyzing all the financial news and trying to
calculate when he wanted to enter the market. He just... entered the market steadily and
consistently.

Steady Sam didn't even bother with having a separate savings account with his $500 monthly
contribution. He just put it directly into an S&P 500 index fund, every single month, for
the past 35 years. It didn't matter if 1987's Black Friday dented the market by 27%. It
didn't matter that the first COVID lockdowns kicked the market down 9% and 15% more last
year. As far as Steady Sam was concerned, that just meant the market was on sale, as his
$500 would just go toward a larger quantity of discounted shares.

Without making any attempt to time the market, by simply riding out all the ups and downs
over the past 35 years, surely he couldn't have done better than Timing Timmy's perfect
track record?

Wrong.

By the end of the same 35-year period, Steady Sam's portfolio has grown to **$1,192,469.**
His decision to *not* time the market was worth almost $200,000.

## The final takeaway
The moral of this story is: it's not about *when* you time the market, but *how much
time* you're in the market. In spite of the massive downturns Steady Sam encountered,
he had built up a strong fundamental base that rallied back even stronger when the
market was bullish. So now the answer to the question of "when is the best time to time
the stock market" is... "yes".
